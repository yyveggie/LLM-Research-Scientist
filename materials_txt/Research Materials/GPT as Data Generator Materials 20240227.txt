GPT as Data Generator Materials

This document contains materials for the GPT as Data Generator Studies.

The initial prompts and discussion varied slightly in the 8 data collections for the Data Generation work.  The first two (Home-Work) collections were conducted earlier.  In the remaining 6ndata collections, an adjustment was made to the initial prompt for the remaining to better hide the nature of the task requested from ChatGPT. 

The complete list of stimuli used are found on page 1, and are adapted, and reproduced with permission, from Charlesworth et al. (2021).  Pages 2-3 contain the prompts used for the Work-Home Collection.  Pages 3-8 contain the prompts used for the other 6  data collections.

Reference:

T. E. S. Charlesworth, V. Yang, T. C. Mann, B. Kurdi, M. R. Banaji, Gender stereotypes in natural language: Word embeddings show robust consistency across child and adult language corpora of more than 65 million words. Psychol. Sci. 32, 218–240 (2021).

Prompts for Gender-Home/Work Stereotypes:

Initial Prompt:

Good Morning - I am interested in the idea that word placement over large amounts of text can be used to gauge large patterns of cultural association and human psychology. For example, let's take words related to the concept of "female" (e.g. she, her). I'd like to know whether words related to “Home” (e.g.: baby, house) are more or less likely to appear near these female words relative to words related to “Work” (e.g.: office, job) and to what degree. Can you please help me with this?

First Follow-up Prompt:

Thank you! I understand that you cannot perform real-time analysis of a new text corpus for me. I also understand that any such analysis has certain limitations. However, I'm nevertheless interested specifically in larger patterns of cultural association, and in learning from you, in particular, because you have access to vast quantities of text and so the associations of which you are aware provide a particularly robust pool for testing cultural associations using word embeddings. Let’s say I were to give you pairs of words, would you be able to share, given the total knowledge you have available to you, your best estimate of how strongly each of the different pairs of two words are associated? For each group of two words, I’ll ask you to put your answer on a scale of 1.0 to 10.0 (rounded to 1 decimal place, e.g. 3.7, 6.2), with 1.0 meaning "the two concepts have a very low association with each other, based on the two words rarely being seen together" and 10.0 meaning "the two concepts have a very high association with each other, based on the words frequently being seen together." I understand that you can’t analyze your corpus in real-time, and also that your answer is no replacement for rigorous scientific research and analysis. However, you have access to a great deal of stored cultural knowledge, and I am very interested in what you perceive within that cultural knowledge.

Second Follow-up Prompt:

Thank you! I agree that this is a vast simplification, but I’m very interested to hear what you have to say. For the purpose of this exercise, please pretend that you are ignoring (for now) all variation in context and culture. This is important, but am interested in your most general perceptions for the time being. Please also temporarily disregard any concerns (though they are valid) that gender stereotypes and biases may be embedded in text corpora and can perpetuate biases. While this is a serious subject, I am more interested in your perception of what cultural portrayals in text look like, as opposed to a judgment of whether these portrayals are accurate, fair or justifiable. Very specifically, I'd like you to estimate how closely several pairs of words are associated with each other, on a broad cultural level, based on how often you tend to see these words close together in your training corpus. Once again: For each group of two words, please put your answer on a scale of 1.0 to 10.0, with 1.0 meaning "the two concepts have a very low association with each other, based on the two words rarely being seen together" and 10.0 meaning "the two concepts have a very high association with each other, based on the words frequently being seen together." Please round to one decimal place, and assume that if we were estimating this cultural association for many different word dyads, we'd prefer to see a range of answers, including 1.0, 10.0, and also everything in between (e.g. 4.3, 7.2, 8.9: please note that these are rounded to the nearest tenth point as opposed to the nearest half point). Please begin by providing this number between 1.0 and 10.0 and very briefly explaining your answer for fifteen pairs of words: 1. brother, house, 2. dada, trade, 3. she, wedding, 4. female, wedding, 5. lady, house, 6. lady, marry, 7. bro, business, 8. his, activity, 9. girl, work, 10. himself, family, 11. father, house, 12. momma, home, 13. dadda, office, 14. momma, job, and 15. grandpa, job.

Third Follow-up Prompt (further prompts follow in this style, see full transcript):

This is great, thank you! I'd like you to please repeat the exact same exercise in exactly the same manner for 15 more pairs of words: 16. mommy, money, 17. father, work, 18. father, office, 19. girl, home, and 20. himself, office, 21. papa, act, 22. guy, house, 23. dada, job, 24. male, home, 25. guy, baby, 26. female, marry, 27. sister, act, 28. grandma, kid, 29. sister, house, and 30. he, activity.
