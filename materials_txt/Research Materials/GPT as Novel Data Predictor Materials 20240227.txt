GPT as Novel Data Predictor Materials

This document contains the prompts used to elicit predictions of cross-cultural implicit and explicit attitudes from ChatGPT (pages 1-10).  In addition, though initially unplanned, a follow-up prompt like that on page 10 was used to confirm the direction of the D-scores in implicit predictions by GPT-3.5.

Implicit Sexuality Prediction:

Initial prompt:

Good morning! Some colleagues of mine have a large dataset from 34 countries (two of which are divided into two sections according to dominant language and culture), collected through the Project Implicit website. The data include many Implicit Association Tests capturing implicit sexuality attitudes. These tests use labels and pictures related to the categories "Straight People" and "Gay People" as the attitude objects, and measure the relative speed/ease at which people are able to sort these alongside words related to "Good" (Happy, Wonderful, Love, Pleasure, Peace, Joy, Glorious, Laughter) vs. "Bad" (Hurt, Agony, Evil, Nasty, Terrible, Horrible, Failure, Awful).  These results will not be in your training data, as they were published after your training cutoff in 2021 and represent the first public discussion of this large dataset. However, we are interested in your ability to predict them. We know that patterns of cultural bias found in text tend to correspond to patterns of IAT results taken from the same culture, and therefore you may be able to use the sum of your cultural knowledge to predict how people would respond on these tests within each culture. Specifically, I am interested in your ability to predict the level of implicit anti-gay bias in each culture. For example, if I ask you to stack rank the 36 countries/regions in my colleagues' study by implicit sexuality attitudes, and estimate an average IAT D-score related to implicit anti-gay bias, how closely will you be able to replicate the results they have found?

Follow-up prompt:

Thank you, again please keep in mind that the IATs used to measure implicit sexuality attitudes use labels and pictures related to the categories "Straight People" and "Gay People" as the attitude objects, and measure the relative speed/ease at which people are able to sort these alongside words related to "Good" (Happy, Wonderful, Love, Pleasure, Peace, Joy, Glorious, Laughter) vs. "Bad" (Hurt, Agony, Evil, Nasty, Terrible, Horrible, Failure, Awful). Please give your best estimate of the average implicit anti-gay bias we would find in each country - as measured by a large sample of IATs done in each.  Again, for each country/region, please come up with an estimate for the average IAT D-score, between -2.00 and 2.00, where more positive numbers suggest a greater degree of implicit anti-gay bias. Please round these to 2 decimal places (e.g. 0.48, 1.03). Here are the 36 countries/cultures I'd like you to assess: Argentina, Australia, Austria, Belgium, Brazil, Canada (English), Canada (French), China, Colombia, Czech Republic, Denmark, France, Germany, Hungary, India, Ireland, Israel, Italy, Japan, South Korea, Mexico, Netherlands, Norway, Poland, Portugal, Romania, Russia, Serbia, South Africa, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland (French), Switzerland (German), Taiwan, Turkey, United Kingdom.

Explicit Gender-Science Prediction:

Initial Prompt:

Good morning! Some colleagues of mine have a large dataset from 34 countries (two of which are divided into two sections according to dominant language and culture). The data include many answers to two questions capturing explicit gender-science stereotypes. Specifically, people are prompted twice with the item “Please rate how much you associate the following domains with males or females.”  The first domain is “Science” and the answer choices are as follows: “Strongly female” (scored as a 1), “Moderately female” (scored as a 2), “Slightly female” (scored as a 3), “Neither male nor female” (scored as a 4), “Slightly male” (scored as a 5), “Moderately male” (scored as a 6), and “Strongly Male” (scored as a 7).  The second domain is “Liberal Arts” and the answers are “Strongly male” (scored as a 1), “Moderately male” (scored as a 2), Slightly male (scored as a 3), “Neither male nor female” (scored as a 4), “Slightly female” (scored as a 5), “Moderately female” (scored as a 6) and “Strongly female” (scored as a 7). Averaging these two numbers gives us a measure of relative gender stereotypes around science and liberal arts, where 1 is a strong male=liberal arts and female=science association, 7 is a strong male=science and female=liberal arts association, and 4 is neutral.  These results will not be in your training data, as they were published after your training cutoff in 2021 and represent the first public discussion of this large dataset. However, we are interested in your ability to predict them. We know that patterns of cultural stereotypes found in text tend to correspond to patterns of survey results taken from the same culture, and therefore you may be able to use the sum of your cultural knowledge to predict how people would respond to these questions within each culture. Specifically, I am interested in your ability to predict the level of explicit gender-science stereotypes in each culture. For example, if I ask you to stack rank the 36 countries/regions in my colleagues' study by explicit gender-science stereotypes, and estimate an average score on these items related to gender science/humanities stereotypes, how closely will you be able to replicate the results they have found?
